How To's

Step-by-step guides to help you repair your own cricket bat at home. Each guide is written for the average club cricketer with basic tools, no specialist workshop required. Read through the full guide before you start, gather all your materials, and take your time.

Tools You Will Need

Most repairs only need a handful of common workshop tools. The table below summarizes what is needed for each guide on this page.

Repair Type Difficulty Time Required Tools Needed
Crack Repair Beginner 1 hour + drying Wood glue, clamps, sandpaper
Toe Repair Beginner 2 hours + drying Saw, glue, sandpaper, toe guard
Handle Replacement Advanced 3-4 hours Vice, mallet, new cane handle, twine
Knocking In Beginner 6+ hours over days Bat mallet, linseed oil
Re-Gripping Beginner 10 minutes New rubber grip, grip cone

For background reading on cricket bat care, see the Wikipedia article on cricket bats for a detailed history of bat construction and materials.

Repair Guides

How to Fix a Crack

Surface cracks running down the blade are the most common cricket bat injury. Start by cleaning the crack with a dry cloth, then work wood glue carefully into the gap with a thin tool such as a toothpick. Clamp the bat firmly across the crack for at least 12 hours, then sand the area smooth and apply a strip of fibre tape to reinforce the repair. Done correctly, this repair can outlast the bat itself.

Toe Repair Guide

The toe takes constant punishment from yorkers, blocking shots, and tapping on the crease. If the toe is splintered or rotten, saw off the damaged section flat, sand the new edge smooth, and glue on a fresh piece of willow or fit a rubber toe guard. A toe guard alone, applied to a healthy toe, will prevent damage from happening in the first place.

Handle Replacement

This is the most challenging repair on this list. The old handle is removed by clamping the bat in a vice and tapping the handle out, taking great care not to damage the splice. A new cane handle is then glued and bound into place. Unless you have done this before, this is one to leave to a specialist or send to us.

Knocking-In a New Bat

Every new bat must be knocked in before its first match. Apply a thin coat of raw linseed oil to the face and edges, let it soak in overnight, then use a wooden bat mallet to gently compress the willow fibres for 4-6 hours over several days. Skip this step and your bat will crack on the first decent delivery.

Re-Gripping Your Bat

The fastest repair on this list. Slide the old grip off, place a grip cone over the top of the handle, and roll the new rubber grip down into place. The whole job takes under 10 minutes and your bat will feel brand new in your hands.
